Indoor Environmental Quality (IEQ) is concerned with how 4 aspects impact occupant health and wellbeing:

  1. Acoustics
  2. Indoor air quality
  3. Lighting
  4. Thermal comfort

Out of these, thermal comfort, the satisfaction occupants feel towards their thermal environment, is considered the most important. Thermal comfort affects morale, productivity and health. Hence, there are several standards and guidelines for IEQ, including ASHRAE 55.

As SAMBA is a valuable IEQ data collecting tool, all of the data variables in this experience averages SAMBA data from different offices and floors across Sydney, hence presenting an overview of the typical office IEQ.

The slider you just completed is known as Predicted Mean Vote (PMV).

PMV is an important measure of IEQ, as it pertains to the measure of percieved human comfort and hence can be used to predict productivity.

A -3 value is too cold and a +3 value is too hot. A 0 value is perfect, which occurs when your heat production and heat loss are equal, and is referred to as thermal equilibrium. To comply with ASHRAE 55, PMV must not exceed a Â±0.5 value.

Mean radiant temperature refers to the heat radiating from a person or object and out into their immediate environment. Mean radiant temperature and air temperature are strongly correlated, and in turn, air temperature is strongly related to PMV. This is often why radiant temperature is assumed to equal the room temperature.

You can see in the graph above, that in the winter months, the mean radiant temperature is slightly higher than the air temperature, as during this time the body temperature of occupants is increased through automatic physiological behaviours.

Lower air speed does not affect the actual air temperature, or the radiant temperature, but it does affect the perceived temperature. Additionally, lower air speed may cause fatigue and contributes to a higher PMV, although not as significantly as the other factors.
